# What are the different types of reporting and accountability a control agency or funder might require from an initiative?

> **Pair:** Question (this page) · [Tool](e1tool.md)

A control agency or purchaser/funder may require different types of reporting and accountability from an agency, provider, policy, organization or initiative. This Types of Reporting and Accountability a Control Agency or Purchaser/Funder Can Require From an Initiative Tool (E1) can be used to specify what is being required of an initiative.

The DoView Planning Diagram (D1) shows the nine components of planning, monitoring, and evaluation that can be used with any initiative. A central agency/purchaser/funder can get an initiative to do any of these components; it can do some of them itself; or it can get other parties to do them. Component A sets out the initiative's strategy and rationale, Component B identifies priorities and Component C shows alignment between outcomes and projects/activities. These three can be done using DoView Planning tools and usually (but not always) a central agency/purchaser/funder will get an initiative to do these itself.

Component D measures controllable indicators (e.g. outputs) attributable to the initiative just by reason of having been measured. Almost all initiatives are required to report on these themselves. Component E is not-necessarily controllable indicators (e.g. outcomes). An initiative may, or may not, have to report on these because others (e.g. in the case of health, a national health statistics agency) may already report on them. Component F is what the initiative is doing to improve its implementation and performance. The more novel an initiative, the more a control agency/purchaser/funder will require this from either the initiative or an outside quality assurance or accreditation body. Component G is process evaluation, describing the course and context of the initiative to help interpret impact evaluation findings and replicate the initiative. Sometimes a funder will get outside evaluators to do this. Component H is impact evaluation, which a control agency/purchaser/funder may, or may not, require that an initiative, or a third party, does. Component I is economic evaluation and summative evaluation; again, a control agency/purchaser/funder may, or may not, get an initiative to do this. See the DoView Planning Framework (D1) for more details on the components used in this tool.
