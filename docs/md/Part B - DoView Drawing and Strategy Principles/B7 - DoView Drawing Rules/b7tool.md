# DoView Tool B7 — DoView Drawing Rules

> **Pair:** [Question](b7question.md) · Tool (this page)

Use this set of rules to build a drill-down strategy/outcomes diagram to ensure that it is fit-for-purpose for a range of tasks. You can use this set of rules to justify to diagram building workshop participants how you are building the diagram.

### Easy to read

1. Keep the names of the boxes in your DoView diagram as short as possible for quick reading.
2. Use outcomes language, not activities or process language - change the wording from '...ing' to '...ed' (e.g. from 'increasing stakeholder support' to 'increased stakeholder support').
3. Only include a single step in each box (i.e. do not put 'achieve A by doing B' in a single box, turn this into two separate boxes) to allow for the consideration of alternative ways of achieving A.

### 'World-centric' not 'program-centric' models

4. Do not assume that an organization needs a single high-level outcome, if it is working on multiple issues it might need multiple high-level outcomes.
5. Do not just limit the boxes in your diagram to things that the agency, provider, policy or initiative controls. They should be 'world-centric' diagrams and can include broader boxes like assumptions and risks relevant to achieving outcomes (word risks positively e.g. 'wider economic situation will remain stable').
6. Do not just restrict yourself to currently quantifiable steps and outcomes. Put measures (indicators) into your DoView diagram separately, positioned next to the boxes they measure and markup which are controllable (in controllables-only contacting these are your direct accountabilities - Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)).
7. Do not siloize your DoView. Let lower-level boxes potentiall influence more than one higher-level box.
8. Structure your DoView based on the general flow of 'This-Then' causality from left to right. If you wish, markup the boxes in the diagram with labels like: inputs, outputs, intermediate outcomes and final outcomes AFTER the diagram has been drawn. Do not interrupt the flow of causality by forcing boxes into columns under these labels. This means the 'This-Then' causal flow will be clearer to readers.
9. Future-proof your DoView by making it generic and including all of the major strategies for achieving outcomes (e.g. education, regulation, role-models etc.). Map your current priorities onto it (so when priorities change you will not need to change your DoView because other options are already in it.

### Well-structured

10. Put outcomes into left-to-right order using the rule that: 'B' Goes to the right of 'A' where 'B' is an 'end' and 'A' is a 'means'. Test this by checking that, if 'B' magically appeared you would not bother doing 'A'. But if 'A' magically appeared, you would still want to do 'B'.
11. Include all relevant necessary steps at each level. Ask yourself if you have 'all the things that need to be done' in order to move to the next column of boxes in your diagram.
12. If appropriate, put a descriptor such as 'adequate', 'sufficient', 'high-quality' in front of your steps and outcomes (e.g. 'sufficient high-quality services') to make sound more like outcomes.
13. Make your diagram as large as you like by dividing it up into drill-down subpages that are meaningful to readers (these drill-down pages can be divided into areas such as: national, regional, sector collaboration, organizational and individual).
