# How can DoView strategy/outcomes diagrams potentially be used to build AI agents and systems?

> **Pair:** Question (this page) · [Tool](j02tool.md)

AI can be used to support DoView Planning by drawing DoViews strategy/outcomes diagrams using our AI DoView Drawing Prompt, identifying KPIs and indicators, traffic lighting progress and doing DoView Visual Alignment. But, in addition to this, DoView Planning can be applied to the management, alignment and control of AI systems themselves.

DoView is an applied form of outcomes theory, a generic, agent-neutral framework for planning, implementing, measuring, and managing any action, whether carried out by humans, human/AI hybrid action, or fully autonomous AI. In each case, an agent pursues a chain of outcomes through planned steps; the clearest way to show that "This-Then" logic is a DoView diagram.

Such DoView diagrams make intentions explicit. If humans are to oversee AI, we first need a transparent statement of what an AI has been designed to achieve and how it should try to achieve it. DoView gives a compact, human-readable map that can serve as a reference design for alignment and governance. The same format can also orchestrate complex collaborations between human and AI agents working together toward shared outcomes.

Of course, describing AI's desired behavior is only the start. As with human initiatives, we must measure and audit whether an AI's real-world actions match what is in its DoView diagram. Given AI's opacity, this oversight will likely require secondary AI tools that monitor, test, and certify alignment of an AI with its DoView.

Many classic DoView Planning and outcomes theory issues reappear in AI management: delegating outcome, choosing performance indicators, distinguishing controllable from uncontrollable indicators, designing outcomes-based incentives at the right level of controllability, and deciding which trade-offs an AI may make autonomously. Many DoView Planning tools in this book may be able to be adapted for use with AI systems. This makes sense because the underlying logic of taking action conceptualized by outcomes theory does not differ just because different agents (human or AI) are undertaking it.
